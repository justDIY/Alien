Day1

Land Bologna → drive to Siena → first evening in the medieval city

Thursday, May 22 · The long arrival day · must land by 11am
🚗 Bologna → Siena · 2hrs · A1 motorway⚠ Land by 11am
Travel + Siena
Land
Arrive Bologna Marconi Airport (BLQ)
BLQ is small and efficient — immigration and baggage typically 45–60min. Pick up your two pre-booked rental cars. Load 8 people and 10 days of luggage. The drive south to Siena is 2 hours of clean motorway on the A1 autostrada — no complexity, no narrow roads, no stress. Leave the airport by 12:30pm at the latest.
The timing gate: Land by 11am → clear customs by 12pm → drive by 12:30pm → Siena by 2:30–3pm, leaving a real afternoon in the city. If you land later than 11am, the afternoon collapses and the whole value of Day 1 is lost. Check your flight arrival time before committing to this plan.
~3:00pm
Arrive Siena — park and check in
Park in Parcheggio Il Campo or Santa Caterina — large underground garages just outside the medieval walls, €2/hr or negotiate a daily rate. Walk through the city gates into the pedestrianised historic centre. Your hotel is inside the walls. Siena's centro is completely car-free — this is the last time you see the vehicles until Day 3. Drop bags, change, have a coffee at the nearest bar. You are now standing in a perfectly preserved 13th-century city.
Hotels inside the Siena walls: Grand Hotel Continental on Banchi di Sopra — the main street, frescoed interiors, central location, the best choice. Palazzo Ravizza — quieter, beautiful garden, near Porta Laterino. Hotel Athena — reliable, good value, 10min from the Campo. All have parking agreements with nearby garages.
4:30pm
Piazza del Campo — the greatest medieval square in Italy
Walk directly to the Campo. The shell-shaped square — uniquely sloped like a theatre, surrounded by 13th-century palaces on every side — is unlike anything you've seen. Sit on the slope (everyone does; the design invites it). Order Aperol Spritz for the adults, something cold for the kids. Look at the Torre del Mangia rising 88m at the far end. This is where the Palio horse race happens — the most intense civic ritual in Europe, run continuously since 1283. You can feel the arena quality of the space even when it's empty. Gio will want to run from one side to the other. Let him.
Designed 1297 · shell shape represents the Virgin Mary's cloak · UNESCO World Heritage
5:30pm
Siena Duomo — exterior and brief interior
5min walk from the Campo. The black and white striped marble facade — Gothic gold mosaics, carved prophets, 300 years of accumulated decoration — is one of Italy's most striking. Go inside for 20min: the striped interior, the inlaid marble floor (56 narrative panels across the entire floor surface, partially visible year-round), and Nicola Pisano's extraordinary pulpit. Peek through the door of the Piccolomini Library — 15th century frescoes in perfect vivid colour, free to view from the entrance. You'll see enough today to understand why tomorrow's Florence comparison is interesting.
€5 basic entry · late afternoon, fewer crowds
7:00pm
Contrade walk — Siena's living medieval neighbourhoods
Walk any direction from the Campo and find the 17 contrade neighbourhood symbols painted on street corners — the Eagle, the Wolf, the Porcupine, the Goose, the Caterpillar. Each neighbourhood has its own church, museum, fountain, and centuries-old civic identity. Ask at the hotel which contrada you're in. The staff will tell you immediately — and then tell you which contrada is the enemy. This is not historical re-enactment. It is alive.
Spot the animal symbols on street corners — the best scavenger hunt in Tuscany
8:00pm
Dinner — Siena
La Taverna di San Giuseppe: underground cave dining room carved into medieval rock beneath the city — one of the most atmospheric restaurants in Tuscany. Book ahead. Or Trattoria Papei on Piazza del Mercato, just behind the Campo — family-friendly, outdoor tables on a beautiful quiet square, excellent local food. First Sienese order: pici al cinghiale — thick hand-rolled pasta with wild boar ragù, the defining dish of this region. Cantucci biscuits with Vin Santo to finish: dip the almond biscuit into the sweet wine. This is the Tuscan ritual.
Pici al cinghiale · ribollita · bistecca · cantucci + Vin Santo
Day2

Florence — David, Ponte Vecchio & Piazzale Michelangelo

Friday, May 23 · Day trip from Siena · 1h 20min each way · back for dinner
🚗 Siena → Florence · 1h 20min · SS2 or superstrada
Florence
8:30am
Drive Siena → Florence — 1h 20min
Take the Siena–Firenze superstrada (dual carriageway, no toll, fast). Park at Piazzale Michelangelo (large lot, ~€3/hr) before descending into the city. Do NOT drive into central Florence — ZTL cameras issue automatic fines to any non-resident vehicle in the historic centre. Walk or take a taxi from the parking area down to the Accademia.
Park at Piazzale Michelangelo · never drive into centro · ZTL fines are automatic
10:00am
Piazzale Michelangelo — opening panorama
If you parked here, start with the view before descending. The panoramic terrace above the city: the Duomo's dome, the Palazzo Vecchio tower, the Ponte Vecchio, the Arno. In morning light this is the finest city view in Italy. Take the group photo here — it's the one that goes on the wall at home. Walk down through the rose garden (free, in full May bloom) toward the river.
The group photo that everyone actually uses — make it count
10:45am
Accademia Gallery — Michelangelo's David · 1.5hrs
Pre-booked 10am or 11am entry. The David is 5.17 metres of single-block marble, carved when Michelangelo was 26. The scale in person is the first shock — photographs cannot convey it. The second shock is the subject: Michelangelo chose the moment of decision before David throws the stone, not the victory after. Every muscle is loaded with tension. The veins on the hands are individually carved. Stand in front of it for five minutes without your phone. The hall also holds the Prisoners — four unfinished figures half-emerging from their marble blocks, intentionally left incomplete. More haunting than anything finished.
~€12/adult · galleriaaccademia.it · pre-book essentialTell Alex: Michelangelo was 26 when he finished this. Watch his reaction.
12:30pm
Lunch — Mercato Centrale · 1hr
5min walk from the Accademia. Ground floor: one of Italy's great food markets — meats, cheeses, fresh pasta, truffles. First floor: food hall with 30+ stalls. Perfect for 8 people with different appetites. Gio and Sophie: pasta and the gelato counter upstairs. Alex and Cyril: try the lampredotto (Florentine street food — offal sandwich, a genuine tradition, remarkable if they're adventurous). Adults: fresh pappardelle with truffle. Budget €12–16/person. No reservations, no queue, completely informal.
Best option for 8 people with different tastes · gelato counter on the upper floor is world-class
2:00pm
Ponte Vecchio + Duomo complex + Ghiberti's Gates
Walk to Ponte Vecchio — the medieval bridge lined with goldsmiths' shops, 700 years old. Cross it, look over the parapet at the Arno. Then the Duomo complex: Brunelleschi's dome (the largest brick dome ever built, the engineering of which Brunelleschi never fully explained and took to his grave), the Baptistery where Dante was baptised, and Ghiberti's Gates of Paradise — the east Baptistery doors so perfectly executed that Michelangelo named them. Queue 5min to stand close: ten gilded bronze panels, each a complete Biblical narrative with perfect perspective carved in relief. These doors took 27 years to complete.
Brunelleschi's dome: 1436 · still the largest masonry dome in the world · the technique was a secret
4:00pm
San Lorenzo leather market + gelato
The outdoor leather market around San Lorenzo church: genuine Florentine craft leather — bags, wallets, jackets, belts. All negotiable (start at 70% of asking). Give Alex and Cyril a budget and let them negotiate independently — at 16 and 13 this is exactly the right autonomy. Then Gelateria dei Neri or Vivoli (Florence's oldest, 1930) for the mandatory gelato. Family vote on best flavour of the whole trip so far.
Alex + Cyril negotiate their own leather purchase · life skill
5:30pm
Drive back to Siena — 1h 20min
Return via the Chiantigiana (SS222) if you want the scenic Chianti Classico wine route at golden hour — vineyard estates, cypress avenues, stone farmhouses. Or straight back on the superstrada. Either way, in Siena by 7pm for the final dinner before tomorrow's big drive south.
8:00pm
Final Siena dinner
If you did La Taverna last night, try Osteria Le Logge — a beautiful 19th-century pharmacy converted into a restaurant, wooden cabinets, serious Sienese food. Or Il Carroccio for simpler, excellent value. This is the last evening in Tuscany. Order the bistecca Fiorentina if anyone didn't get it in Florence — this is the correct geography for it. Cantucci with Vin Santo again. Nobody refuses the second round.
Bistecca Fiorentina · pici · pecorino with truffle honey · cantucci + Vin Santo
Day3

Pienza morning → drive south → arrive Amalfi on the sea

Saturday, May 24 · The split day · checkout early · leave Pienza by 1pm
🚗 Siena → Pienza · 45min scenic🚗 Pienza → Amalfi · 3h 45min · A1 + A3 + SS163⚠ Leave Pienza 1pm hard
Transfer
8:00am
Checkout Siena — load cars — be driving by 9am
Bags packed the night before. Checkout by 8:30am. This is the one day where timing matters absolutely — a late start compounds all the way to a dark arrival in Amalfi with tired children and a narrow coastal road still to navigate. The rule is simple: driving out of Siena by 9am.
9:00am
Drive Siena → Pienza via SP146 · 45min
Take the SP146 south from San Quirico d'Orcia — this is the road on every Tuscany postcard. Cypress tree avenues, rolling gold and green hills, lone farmhouses on every ridge. Drive slowly. Stop once for the photograph. The Cappella di Vitaleta — a small Romanesque chapel in an open field approached by a double cypress avenue — is the single most iconic spot in Tuscany. GPS it directly: Cappella della Madonna di Vitaleta, near San Quirico d'Orcia. Five minutes stopped, photographed, continuing. Do not skip it.
45min · stop at Cappella di Vitaleta · the Tuscany postcard moment
10:00am
Pienza — 2.5 hours · the perfect Renaissance town
Pope Pius II demolished and completely rebuilt his birthplace in 1459 as an ideal Renaissance city — and finished it in three years. The result is the most perfectly proportioned small town in Italy. Walk Corso Rossellino to Piazza Pio II. The view from the garden behind the cathedral: a sudden drop 300m straight down the cliff to the Val d'Orcia floor, the landscape extending to Montalcino's ridge on the far side. This view is worth the detour. Buy here: Pecorino di Pienza in every form — fresh, aged, truffle-infused, wrapped in walnut leaves. Taste at the shops on the main street. Local honey. Handmade pasta. This is the best food shopping of the Tuscany section. Quick lunch at any trattoria on the main street.
Buy: Pecorino di Pienza · truffle honey · handmade pici · the best Tuscan food shopping
Hard departure: 1:00pm from Pienza. Amalfi is 3hrs 45min away on the fastest route. Leaving at 1pm means arriving by 5pm — time to check in, see the sea, and decompress. Every 30min you add here arrives as 30min of coastal road in the dark. Enjoy Pienza fully, then leave precisely on time.
1:00pm
Drive Pienza → Amalfi town · 3h 45min
Head to the A1 autostrada via Bettolle junction, south the length of Italy past Rome's orbital, down the A3 past Vesuvius (visible from the motorway, enormous), exit at Castellammare di Stabia. Then the SS163 coastal road — the Amalfi Drive. This final 25km of the SS163 is the famous narrow coastal road. Drive it in daylight, arriving well before dark. Take it slowly. Two cars in convoy, keep distance. The views are extraordinary and the road is genuinely narrow in places. At Amalfi town: follow signs to Parking Luna Rossa — the large multi-storey garage at the town entrance. Park both cars. You will not move them again until Rome departure on Day 6.
A1 + A3 + SS163 · all comfortable until the last 25km coastal section · take it steadyDrive SS163 in daylight · arriving after dark on this road with 8 people is avoidable and should be avoided
~5:00pm
Arrive Amalfi — check in — first look at the sea
Hotel porters collect luggage from the car park — this is standard in Amalfi, all the good hotels have it arranged. Walk to the hotel. Drop bags. Walk directly to the waterfront. The Bay of Salerno in the late afternoon light, the cliffs above, the Duomo steps rising behind you — this is the Amalfi Coast arriving all at once. Gio sees the sea. He will not want to do anything else for the next two days. That's correct.
Why Amalfi town works perfectly for this group: The beach (Spiaggia di Amalfi) is 2min from every good hotel. The town is flat along the waterfront. The ferry dock is 3min from the hotel — for tomorrow's Pompeii day trip and the Positano ferry on Day 6. The Duomo is a 3min walk. In the evening when day-trippers leave by 6pm, the town becomes genuinely Italian — locals at outdoor tables, the smell of limoncello and seafood, the cliffs lit warm by the last light.
7:30pm
First evening stroll + limoncello
Walk the harbour, climb the 62 steps to the Duomo di Amalfi exterior — the Arab-Norman cathedral facade is extraordinary in the evening light, completely different from anything in Tuscany or Rome. Buy limoncello from a proper local producer on the Via Lorenzo d'Amalfi — not the tourist shops near the port but the enotecas slightly further in. The Amalfi lemon is a protected variety, larger and more fragrant than any other. The limoncello made from it is a different drink from what you've had before.
8:30pm
Dinner — first seafood night
Il Tari (excellent local seafood, right in the centre, reliable service), Ristorante La Caravella (one Michelin star, historical, the place for a special dinner — book ahead), or Trattoria Il Mulino for simpler, excellent local food. First coastal order: spaghetti alle vongole — clams, white wine, parsley, the defining dish of this coastline. Fresh grilled branzino. Fried calamari. The delizia al limone for dessert — a dome of sponge and lemon cream that is one of the most accurately named desserts in Italy.
Spaghetti alle vongole · branzino al sale · frittura di paranza · delizia al limone
Day4

Amalfi — beach, town, Duomo & total relaxation

Sunday, May 25 · No driving · No ferries · Nothing mandatory · This is the gift day
Amalfi
9:00am
Late hotel breakfast on the terrace
The hotel terrace with the bay below you. Fresh sfogliatella, local pastries, fruit, the best coffee of the trip. Nobody is in a hurry. This morning exists precisely to do nothing quickly. The sea is already there. Gio has already asked three times when they're going to the beach.
10:00am
Spiaggia di Amalfi — the whole morning
The Amalfi town beach is 2min from the hotel. Beach clubs (sunbeds + umbrella ~€25–30/set) or free sections on the sides and further along. The water is clear, the cliffs rise above, the town behind you. This is what the last 4 days of driving and history have been building toward. Gio goes straight into the water and does not come out until lunch. Sophie snorkels in the rocky edges — bring the mask from home. Alex and Cyril jump off the rocks. The adults order something cold and don't move for three hours. This is the correct use of a beach day on the Amalfi Coast.
Bring snorkel gear from home — the rocky edges are excellent · crystal clear waterBeach clubs: ~€25/set · Free section available on the far side
1:00pm
Lunch — on the waterfront
The restaurants along the Amalfi harbour do excellent fresh seafood. Il Tari again for simplicity, or any of the harbour terraces. Today's lunch is about location: eating fresh pasta al pomodoro with the sea directly below you, the cliffs above, a glass of cold Falanghina (the local white wine) in your hand. Budget €20–28/person.
Pasta al pomodoro · mixed fried seafood · Falanghina bianco · tiramisù
3:00pm
Duomo di Amalfi — the Arab-Norman cathedral
Climb the 62 steps to the cathedral facade — striped black and white marble, Arab-Norman arches, Byzantine mosaics on the facade. This is the most historically layered building on the coast: Amalfi was a major maritime republic before Venice and Genoa, trading with the Arab world throughout the Middle Ages, and the architecture shows it. The Cloister of Paradise (Chiostro del Paradiso) inside: 13th-century Arabic columns, medieval sarcophagi of Amalfi nobles, a garden enclosed within the cloister walls. Genuinely extraordinary and rarely crowded. Budget 45min.
Amalfi was a major maritime republic 839–1073 AD · traded with Byzantines, Arabs, Normans simultaneously~€3 entry to Cloister of Paradise
4:30pm
Valle dei Mulini — the lemon grove walk
From behind the Duomo, a shaded path leads up through working lemon groves to a ruined medieval paper mill. The walk is 20min each way, easy, completely free, and one of the most pleasant things to do in Amalfi. Enormous lemons hang overhead, the valley is cool and green, and the paper mill ruins are genuinely interesting — Amalfi invented a particular type of paper still used for important documents today. Gio picks a lemon off a low-hanging branch. This is allowed and expected.
Easy shaded walk · through actual lemon groves · Gio picks a lemon · completely free
6:30pm
Ceramic shopping + evening passeggiata
Amalfi coast ceramics — hand-painted in the local style (bright blue, yellow, and white, lemons and sea patterns) — are genuinely excellent here. Buy from established workshops, not port stalls. Ceramiche Artistiche on Via Lorenzo d'Amalfi or any studio with artists working visibly inside. Then the evening passeggiata along the harbour: the Italian ritual of walking slowly with no destination, watching the fishing boats come in, the cliffs going pink and orange above.
Granita di limone at a harbour bar: the best thing to drink at this hour in this place
8:30pm
Dinner — second Amalfi night
If budget allows: Ristorante La Caravella tonight — one Michelin star, opened 1959, inside a former boat storage cave, the most serious restaurant on the coast. Historic, beautiful, and genuinely excellent. Book well ahead. Otherwise: Ristorante Eolo for a terrace dinner above the sea. Tonight: the whole grilled orata (gilt-head bream), the linguine all'astice (lobster pasta), the sea urchin if the season allows. Order one of everything and share it all.
La Caravella: the special dinner · book ahead · Michelin starred since 1966
Day5

Pompeii — ferry + train day trip from Amalfi

Monday, May 26 · Morning ruins · afternoon back at the beach · no cars
⛴ Amalfi → Salerno · 35min ferry🚆 Salerno → Pompeii Scavi · 25min train
Pompeii
7:15am
Walk to Amalfi port — ferry to Salerno
Quick bar breakfast at the port (cornetti, cappuccino — 10 minutes). Board the 7:30am ferry from Amalfi to Salerno. Journey: 35min, calm sea, beautiful morning light on the cliffs. Salerno is a proper southern Italian city — not a tourist destination, just a city, and the ferry arrives at a working port. Walk 10min to Salerno Centrale train station, or take a taxi (€5).
Ferry ~€8/person · 35min · departs Amalfi port · check current timetables at traghettiamalfi.it
8:15am
Train Salerno → Pompeii Scavi · 25min
Trenitalia regional train from Salerno Centrale to Pompei (Scavi - Villa dei Misteri) station. ~€3/person. Trains run every 30min. The station drops you directly at the main entrance — no walking, no searching. Pick up your pre-booked tickets at the machine or gate.
Pompeii tickets: pompeiisites.org · 9am entry · ~€18/adult · free under 18 · book in advanceDownload Pompeii Sites app before leaving hotel · best free audio guide available
9:00am
Pompeii Archaeological Park — 3.5 hours
Pompeii is not ruins. It is an entire Roman city — 44 hectares — preserved under volcanic ash since 79 AD. You walk the actual streets, enter houses with frescoes still on the walls, visit bakeries with bread moulds still in the ovens, shops with price lists still painted above the counters. The completeness is what shocks every first-time visitor. The route: Porta Marina entrance → the Forum (start here; Vesuvius is visible at the end of the Forum axis, which Pompeii's urban planners placed deliberately) → Via dell'Abbondanza (the main commercial street, stepping stones still in place between raised pavements) → House of the Faun (the largest private home, the Alexander mosaic) → the Lupanar (the brothel, historically fascinating — the erotic frescoes served as a picture menu for non-Latin-speaking clients from across the Mediterranean) → the Amphitheatre (intact, enormous, the oldest surviving Roman amphitheatre in the world) → Villa dei Misteri (outside the main walls via Porta Ercolano — 10min walk — do NOT skip: the Dionysian frescoes in deep Pompeian red are the finest surviving ancient paintings anywhere).
Destroyed 24 August 79 AD · rediscovered 1748 · only 2/3 excavated · digs still active and finding new roomsAlex 16: this is the single best thing on the entire 10-day trip. Guaranteed.
Garden of the Fugitives — prepare the group: 13 plaster casts of people who died fleeing — preserved in their exact final positions. A family huddled together. A man covering his face. The casts are made by injecting plaster into the hollow spaces the bodies left in the ash. Sobering and profound, not frightening. Sophie (11) and the teenagers: absolutely yes. Gio (6): preview it and decide — most 6-year-olds handle it well when framed as history rather than horror.
12:30pm
Lunch at Pompeii exit + return journey
Simple restaurants just outside the Porta Marina exit — eat quickly, it's hot and everyone needs shade. Train back to Salerno (25min), then ferry Salerno to Amalfi (35min). Back in Amalfi by 3pm. The afternoon is entirely free.
Return: train Pompeii → Salerno · ferry Salerno → Amalfi · total ~1hr 15min
3:00pm
Back in Amalfi — beach afternoon recovery
Pompeii is emotionally and physically intense — the heat, the scale, the history. The beach is the perfect decompression. Gio goes straight to the water. The adults sit in the shade and order cold drinks. Nobody talks about anything serious for at least two hours. This is the intended rhythm: intense morning, gentle afternoon.
7:00pm
Sunset from the harbour wall
Sit on the harbour wall as the day tourists clear out and Amalfi becomes itself again. The cliffs go amber and rose in the late light. The fishing boats are back. Order a granita di limone or a glass of cold white wine. Let the day settle. The conversation will be entirely about Pompeii — let it run.
8:30pm

Rome II — Vatican, St Peter's, Castel Sant'Angelo & Pantheon

8:00am
Vatican Museums + Sistine Chapel · 2.5hrs
Go straight to the Sistine Chapel first — walk against the flow following exit signs. At 8:15am it is genuinely quiet. Stand in the centre and look up. Michelangelo painted almost all of it alone over 4 years (1508–1512). He was 33 when he started. The Creation of Adam is directly above the altar end — find it first, then let your eyes move outward. Then walk back through the Gallery of Maps (the entire Italian peninsula painted on the ceiling in 1580 — stunning and kids find it fascinating) and Raphael's School of Athens. The School of Athens: painted flat but creates a perfect 3D architectural space. Tell the group this is the technique that gave rise to perspective painting in the Renaissance and that cinema uses it today for set extensions. Three rooms done properly beats fifteen rushed.

Castel Sant'Angelo · 1hr
Walk across the angel bridge. Originally Hadrian's mausoleum (135 AD), later a papal fortress — the Pope escaped here via a covered elevated corridor from the Vatican during the 1527 Sack of Rome. The rooftop: the best 360° view in central Rome — Tiber, St. Peter's, the whole city laid out below you.

The Pantheon
20min walk. 125 AD, in continuous use since — converted to a church in 609 AD. The dome's diameter equals its height: 43.3 metres. The oculus — a 9-metre circular hole, the only light source — creates a slowly moving light column on May afternoons. Raphael is buried here. €5. Spend 20 minutes standing and looking up without moving. This is one of the genuinely unrepeatable spatial experiences in architecture.
€5/adult · afternoon light through the oculus is exceptionalLargest unreinforced concrete dome in the world · the Romans' formula was lost for 1,300 years
